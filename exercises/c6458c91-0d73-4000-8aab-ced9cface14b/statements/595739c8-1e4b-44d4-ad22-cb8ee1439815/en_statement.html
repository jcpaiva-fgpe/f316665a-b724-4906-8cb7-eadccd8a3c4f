Prisoner's Dilemma

"Two suspects, A and B, are arrested by the police. The police have evidence insufficient to convict them, but, separating the prisoners, offers the both the same agreement: if one of the prisoners, confessing, testifies against the other and that other remains silent, what he confessed comes out free while the silent accomplice serves 10 years of sentence. If both remain silent, the police can only sentence them to 6 months in jail each one. If they both betray the partner, they each go to jail for 5 years. Each prisoner makes his decision without knowing what decision the other is going to make, and neither is sure of the other's decision. The question that the dilemma poses is: what will happen? How will the prisoner react? "

This is the original formulation of the "Prisoner's Dilemma" made in 1950, and since then used in Game Theory to model such trivial situations, like what two people who accidentally exchange their bags do, or as complex, as the system that became known during the Cold War by Mutual Assured Destruction (MAD).

In Game Theory, the basic models are particularly interesting that allow repeated games, with memory of previous moves. Imagine a card game: the rules of a play can be repeated by several hands; the (good) players remember the cards played previously; and at the end the winnings for each hand are added.

The Prisoner's Dilemma is also a basic game that can be played at any time. iterated form. In each iteration the player's points are given by a earnings matrix. In the classic example presented above the matrix of gains would be as follows (taking prison months as losses):

THE ; B Cooperate Desert
Cooperate -6; -6 -120; 0
Desert 0; -120 -60; -60

The iterated version of the Prisoner's Dilemma models many situations of cooperation and confrontation, from the explanations for the relations living beings based on the Darwinian theory of evolution, the theories economic conditions for relations between companies.

Task

It is intended to write a program to calculate the points of each player Prisoner's Dilemma iterated and determine the winner. How input is given the winning matrix followed by a sequence of n moves. THE matrix is ​​given by lines of 2 values, respectively the gains of the players A and B, for the following cases: A and B cooperate; Cooperate and B deserted; Deserted and B cooperates; A and B defect. A line follows with the n number of moves and n lines with a pair of values ​​0 or 1 (0 defect and 1 cooperate) respectively for players A or B.

The program will indicate the number of points accumulated by each of the players. Each line will have the name of a player (A or B) followed by a space and the respective score, with the players being presented in order decreasing score. If players tie then there will be only one line with reference AB followed by a space and the value of the both.
